Black Holes:

Black holes are regions in space where gravity is so strong that nothing can escape, not even light. They are formed when a large amount of matter is squeezed into a very small area, creating an extremely powerful gravitational pull. Black holes cannot be seen directly because they do not give off light, but scientists can detect them by observing how they affect nearby stars, gas, and light. The boundary around a black hole where nothing can escape is called the event horizon.

Black holes form when very massive stars run out of fuel and collapse under their own gravity. During this process, the star can explode in a huge event called a supernova, leaving behind a small but incredibly dense core. If the core is massive enough, it collapses into a black hole. Some black holes can also grow larger by pulling in gas, dust, and even other black holes.

If you fell into a black hole, the intense gravity would stretch and squeeze your body in a process called "spaghettification." As you got closer to the event horizon, time would appear to slow down compared to someone watching from far away. After crossing the event horizon, you would not be able to escape, and you would eventually be pulled toward the center of the black hole, where scientists believe a singularity may exist.
